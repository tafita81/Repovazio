# Anxious Attachment: Why We Sabotage the Relationships We Most Want

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

Do you check your phone obsessively waiting for a message from someone you care about? Have you sent a follow-up message "just to check" when the first one went unanswered? You're not alone — and you're not irrational. You're likely running an ancient operating system that was written in early childhood.

## What Ainsworth Discovered

In 1969, developmental psychologist Mary Ainsworth conducted what became one of the most cited experiments in psychology: the Strange Situation. She observed infants being briefly separated from and reunited with their caregivers.

Some babies — those who would develop what Ainsworth called anxious attachment — displayed a paradoxical response when their caregiver returned. They would seek comfort but then resist it, alternating between reaching for the parent and pushing them away.

These infants had caregivers who were *inconsistent* — neither neglectful nor abusive, but unpredictable. Sometimes warmly present, sometimes distracted or emotionally unavailable. The infant's brain learned a devastating lesson: love exists, but I cannot count on it.

That lesson doesn't stay in infancy.

## The Neuroscience Behind the Pattern

Daniel Siegel, clinical professor of psychiatry at UCLA, has documented how attachment patterns literally sculpt neural architecture. The amygdala — our threat-detection center — becomes hypervigilant in people with anxious attachment, scanning constantly for signs of abandonment or rejection.

For the anxiously attached brain, an unreturned message isn't just an unreturned message. It's a potential abandonment. The stress response fires with the same intensity as a genuine survival threat — because evolutionarily, social exclusion *was* a survival threat.

This is why reasoning doesn't help in the moment. The logic arrives late. The alarm already fired.

## Five Signs That Aren't Obvious

**Preemptive self-sabotage.** Ending relationships before being left. It's more tolerable to control the pain than to wait for it. The mind calculates: if I leave first, I decide when the loss happens.

**Invisible tests.** Behaviors that seem irrational — picking fights, briefly disappearing, pushing limits — are often unconscious tests. *Will you stay if I act like this? Do you love me enough to remain?* The tragic irony: these tests often cause the partner to leave, confirming the original fear.

**Fusion as safety.** Where the avoidantly attached person needs distance to feel secure, the anxiously attached needs proximity — often extreme proximity. Difficulty having independent hobbies, needing to know the partner's location, anxiety when plans don't include them.

**Microscanning for emotional data.** John Gottman's research shows that anxiously attached individuals are extraordinarily accurate at detecting negative emotions in others' faces — even when presented subliminally. What should be a social skill becomes a source of constant, exhausting alertness.

**The rumination spiral.** The mental replay of conversations. The exhaustive analysis of text messages. The rehearsal of dialogues that never happen. The nervous system doesn't switch off. It works overtime trying to predict and prevent a loss that may not even be coming.

## Why More Love Doesn't Fix It

The cruel paradox: the obvious solution is more reassurance, more evidence of love. But it doesn't work. Or rather, it works briefly. The anxiously attached partner feels better, then the doubt returns, then more reassurance is needed.

Sue Johnson, founder of Emotionally Focused Therapy, describes this clearly: the anxiously attached person isn't responding to the present partner. They're responding to a historical experience, with all the intensity that experience warrants.

The partner isn't the solution because they aren't the source. The source is the learning that happened before the partner existed.

## What Actually Changes Things

**Naming the pattern without identifying with it.** There's a difference between "I am anxious" and "my nervous system is running a high-alert program." One is identity. The other is process — and processes can be interrupted.

**Building tolerance for uncertainty incrementally.** Not checking for a response for two minutes. Then five. Then ten. The nervous system learns through experience that the threat didn't materialize.

**Therapy focused on attachment.** EFT (Sue Johnson), AEDP, and attachment-based CBT have the strongest evidence base for this specific pattern.

The anxious attachment style isn't weakness. It's a nervous system that learned to survive in a specific environment and is still using that map in different territory. You didn't choose it. But you can begin — with time, with support, with real compassion for yourself — to update it.
