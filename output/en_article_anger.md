# Anger Isn't the Problem. What You Do With It Is.

*By Daniela Coelho · Behavioral Research & Psychology*

---

Most people learn about anger in one of two ways: either it should be controlled and suppressed, or it should be "released" and expressed freely.

Both approaches have serious problems.

## What Neuroscience Shows

Anger is an activation emotion. When it arises, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis — our stress response system — is activated. Cortisol and adrenaline elevate heart rate, tense muscles, sharpen focus on threats.

During this activation, the prefrontal cortex — responsible for reasoning, perspective, and impulse control — has reduced access. The window of tolerance narrows.

Daniel Siegel at UCLA calls this state "flipping the lid" — the emotional primitive system takes command.

This explains why things said in anger are often things the person wouldn't have said in another state. It's not "the truth coming out" — it's a different neurobiological state producing different behavior.

## Suppressing Doesn't Resolve

The intuitive solution — holding it in — has documented costs.

James Gross at Stanford studied emotional regulation strategies extensively. Emotional suppression keeps the nervous system activated even without external expression. The anger doesn't go away — it remains physiologically present while expression is blocked.

This produces: subsequent rumination, residual irritability, and — in chronic patterns — correlations with hypertension and immune system compromise.

## "Releasing" Doesn't Either

The cathartic hypothesis — the idea that "letting anger out" brings relief — was extensively tested. The results don't support it.

Brad Bushman at Ohio State conducted studies showing that hitting pillows or yelling when angry frequently increases arousal rather than reducing it. Practicing angry expression increases the tendency toward angry response.

Anger isn't like accumulated pressure that needs releasing. It's like a neural pattern that strengthens with practice.

## What Works

Naming. Matthew Lieberman demonstrated that labeling the emotion — "I'm feeling anger" — reduces amygdala activation. One of the simplest interventions with documented neurological effect.

Physiological pause. Twenty minutes for cortisol to dissipate before responding. Not suppression — just time. The decision made during intense activation is rarely the best one.

Curiosity about the anger. Anger almost always has a deeper layer — fear, sadness, perceived injustice, a violated limit. Identifying what's underneath changes what needs to be addressed.

Assertive expression, not aggressive. "I felt angry when X happened because Y" is different from attacking. The emotion can be communicated without the other person's nervous system needing to defend itself.

---
Anger is information. It points to something that matters — a violated value, a crossed limit, a perceived injustice. The work isn't eliminating it. It's using it.
